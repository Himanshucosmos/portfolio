the burden of infinite choice

2025-09-28 · By Himanshu Sharma

There is a particular kind of paralysis that only the free can know. The prisoner does not agonise over where to go next. He knows exactly where he is going: nowhere. His path is defined by walls. But hand a man a map with no roads on it — just open land in every direction — and watch what happens. He stands at the centre, spinning slowly, going nowhere too. Just for different reasons.

We have inherited the richest menu in history. Career options, lifestyle choices, belief systems, identities — all available, all valid, all competing. The philosophers of the Enlightenment fought for this. They thought freedom was the final answer. But they forgot to ask what happens to a mind that must answer every question about itself, all at once, every day.

Kierkegaard called it "the dizziness of freedom." He meant it literally — the vertigo that comes when you realise the only thing stopping you from being anyone is you. That is supposed to feel like power. Often it just feels like falling.

I used to think indecision was a character flaw. A weakness dressed up in the language of open-mindedness. I have since changed my view. Indecision is what happens when the stakes are real and every path costs something real. You cannot have the bartending shift and the 9-to-5 boardroom ambition on the same Tuesday night. You cannot be the DJ and the systems engineer simultaneously, not without something bleeding somewhere.

The ancient Stoics had a quiet answer to all this. They called it amor fati — love of fate. Not resignation. Not passive acceptance. But a radical embrace of the specific life you are living, including its constraints. A wall is not only a prison. It is also a boundary that tells you exactly where you stand.

I think we have confused abundance with meaning. We assumed that more options would produce more satisfaction. Instead, we got more regret. Because every door you walk through closes seventeen others behind you — and in an age where everything is visible and documented and Instagrammable, you can see every door you didn't take, lit up and glowing, curated by someone who looks like they made the right call.

But here is the thing nobody tells you about the people who look certain: they are not free of doubt. They have simply decided that the cost of doubt is higher than the cost of commitment. They chose. Not because they were sure. Because they were tired of standing at the centre of the map, spinning.

Choice is not a gift you receive. It is a muscle you train. And like all muscles, it atrophies when unused and aches when first put to work. The way out of the burden is not fewer options — the world will not cooperate with that wish. The way out is to choose something, anything, and be honest about the fact that you are choosing, not discovering. There is no destined path waiting to be found. There is only the path you decide to walk, and the story you tell about it later.

Per Aspera, Ad Astra. Through the hardship of infinite possibility — to something that is finally, unmistakably, yours.
