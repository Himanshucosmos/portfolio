suffering as curriculum

2025-11-14 · By Himanshu Sharma

Nobody signs up for the difficult years. They arrive unannounced, usually at the worst possible time, often in multiples. And while they are happening, there is no sense to them — no discernible lesson, no narrative arc you can point to and say: this is where I grow. There is only the problem in front of you, and the question of whether you will still be standing when it passes.

I have slept four hours a night for long enough that it stopped feeling like sacrifice and started feeling like personality. I have worked jobs that paid just enough to make the next week's problem slightly smaller than this week's. I have sat in rooms where the only thing between me and a very bad outcome was the performance I was about to give. I am not telling you this to impress you. I am telling you this because I used to think it was exceptional. It is not. Half the world is living some version of this story right now.

What is interesting to me is not the suffering itself. Suffering is not interesting. It is dumb and indifferent and it happens to everyone in some form. What is interesting is what it does to a person's epistemology — their model of how the world works.

Comfort produces theory. Hardship produces knowledge. Not wisdom necessarily — suffering alone does not grant wisdom, which is why some of the most traumatised people I know are also the most rigid and closed. But it does produce a very particular kind of knowing: the knowledge that you have already survived something you thought would break you. That knowledge is non-transferable. You cannot read it in a book or absorb it from a mentor. It has to be earned in the only currency the universe accepts — time and endurance.

The Stoics had a phrase: premeditatio malorum. The premeditation of evils. The practice of imagining, in advance, everything that could go wrong — not to catastrophise, but to prepare. Marcus Aurelius did this. He would wake up each morning and remind himself that the day ahead contained difficulty, ingratitude, and the full catalogue of human failure. Not to become cynical. But to become unshockable. Because the person who is unshockable can keep moving when everyone else has stopped to stare.

There is a version of self-improvement discourse that treats pain as a vending machine. Insert hardship, receive wisdom. But it does not work that way. The curriculum of suffering has no guaranteed graduation. Some people go through extraordinary difficulty and come out smaller — more fearful, more bitter, less willing to try. The difference, I think, is not in what happened to them. It is in whether they allowed the experience to become information, or whether they locked it away as evidence that the world is against them.

The question you have to ask — not once, but continuously — is: what is this teaching me? Not in a toxic-positive, silver-lining way. Genuinely. What is the actual data here? What have I learned about my own limits? What have I learned about what I can endure, what I need, what I was wrong about before this started?

I do not believe suffering is noble. I do not think hardship is something to romanticise or seek out. But I do believe that the people who have been genuinely tested, and who have chosen to extract something from the test, carry a kind of groundedness that is rare and valuable. They are not afraid of the next hard thing in the same way. Not because they are brave. Because they already know how the hard thing feels from the inside, and they know they came out the other side of the last one.

That is not a lesson. That is a memory. And memory, more than anything else, is what builds a person.
